Your decision — do we name Reta 3?

The upside

We already rank #3 for it with no on-page mention. The searcher's intent is unambiguous — “was this thing really on the show?” — and an 18.8% click-through says they are hunting for a straight answer. A page that actually answers it would likely take position 1–2 and hold this traffic properly instead of catching the spill.

The two constraints I will not cross without you saying so

1. It is a competitor's brand name. Under German UWG §4/§6 we may publish an objective, verifiable comparison, but we may not disparage them. “Reta 3 is a scam, buy RetaFit instead” is off the table — same rule as the UK CAP 3.41 line in our notes, where truth is not a defence.

2. The honest answer cuts both ways. The documented fact (Mimikama, Verbraucherzentrale, VOX) is that no weight-loss product has ever appeared on Die Höhle der Löwen — every such ad is fake. That applies to RetaFit too. Our TV pages are deliberately neutral today: no confirm, no deny. Publishing the straight answer means openly stating it about our own product as well.

My read: do it, and state it about ourselves too. It is the trust play, it is defensible, and it is what the 18.8% click-through is actually asking for. But it trades away the ambiguity our TV pages currently sit on — so it is your call, not mine.

The risk nobody should ignore

This traffic is borrowed, and borrowed traffic ends

Half our clicks depend on somebody else's ad spend on a fad brand. Our own rule of thumb from the Coolizi work puts the life of a viral fad brand at four to six weeks. Reta 3's wave started days ago. When it stops, roughly half this traffic goes with it, and nothing we did will have caused either the rise or the fall.

The durable asset is the category, not the brand: wegovy piller pris at position 4.6, reta medicin, reta kosten, reta kaufen schweiz at position 3.0. Those are small today and they are ours.

Still waiting on you

Three items from the 21 Jul audit are still untouched, deliberately. They are the ones that carry real exposure:

  1. Self-serving Product.review on 9 pages. We are the brand's affiliate, and Google's review-snippet policy excludes reviews by the seller or its affiliate. Rich Results Test still passes — that is exactly why it looks fine. The exposure is a structured-data manual action that would erase the rich results we now hold on all 20 pages. Fix is to drop the review node and keep offers.
  2. Struck-through anchor prices are invented and the savings maths is wrong. £229/£152 match no real price. Five bottles at £73.85 is £369.25, not £229. Copy claims “saves £100” where the true gap is £240.25. Under UK CPRs and the EU Omnibus directive a struck reference price must be a genuine former price. Denmark is the only geo that is currently correct.
  3. Five gold stars shown against a 4.7 rating on 10 pages, plus “loved across the UK & EU” when no real testimonials exist.

Item 2 is the one I would fix first — it is the precise pattern that puts a domain on a Verbraucherzentrale list, and Germany is now 70% of our traffic.
