1. The prices — this was the risky one

Every bundle showed a crossed-out “was” price. None of them were real. They had been reverse-engineered from the store's own percentage claims — £99 ÷ 0.65 = £152, £129 ÷ 0.56 = £229 — so they matched no price anyone was ever charged. Under UK consumer law and the EU Omnibus directive, a crossed-out price must be a genuine former price. That is the precise pattern that gets a site put on a German consumer-protection list, and Germany is now 70% of our traffic.

What replaced it is both legal and a stronger offer

Instead of inventing a former price, the page now compares against something real and checkable: what five single bottles actually cost on the same store.

Old: £129.00  £229  · “Save 44%”  ← the £229 never existed
New: £129.00 · £25.80/bottle · 5 single bottles cost £369.25

The genuine saving is £240.25 — not the £100 we were claiming, and far better than the “44%” the store advertises. Being honest here made the offer look better, not worse.

2. The Reta 3 page

Live at tryretafit.com/de/reta-3/. It answers the question in the first screen, because that is what the searcher came for, and only then talks about us.

What it says

That no weight-loss product has ever appeared on Die Höhle der Löwen — and that this applies to RetaFit too. It says so in plain German, on our own site. Then it explains the name confusion, lists six ways to spot a faked TV ad, and shows our real prices.

How I kept it legal

Reta 3 is described only as “a product from another provider”, and the page explicitly declines to review it. No scam accusation, no comparison table, no claims about their website. German competition law lets you compare objectively but not disparage — and here truth is not a defence, so the safe move was to stay factual and let the reader draw conclusions.

I also swept the two other German pages that still said “we neither confirm nor deny” and made them say the same straight thing, so we do not contradict ourselves.

What I did not do
