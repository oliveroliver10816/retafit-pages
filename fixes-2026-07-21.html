2 · A blocker that would have cost you money on the paid launch

Google click IDs were being thrown awayfixed

Six files read gbraid / wbraid — the click IDs Google sends instead of gclid on iOS and other privacy-restricted traffic — but nothing ever saved them. Two things broke on every one of those clicks:

① The conversion was tagged with the generic organic label instead of the click ID, so it could not be uploaded back into Google Ads — that traffic would have looked like it converted nothing.

② The popup script classified those paid visitors as organic, which switches on the aggressive mode: back-button hijacking and a popup that re-fires on every exit attempt. That is a landing-page disapproval risk, aimed squarely at the $500/day campaign that is queued to launch.

One line to fix. Proven end to end: a visit with ?gbraid=TESTGB123 now produces ?sub1=TESTGB123, while organic visitors still correctly produce ?sub1=retafituk.

3 · Illegal health claims on two markets

Danish and Norwegian were making fat-burning claimsfixed

English, German and French all correctly say fat metabolism. Danish and Norwegian had drifted to fedtforbrænding and fettforbrenning — literal fat-burning — which is an unauthorised health claim under EU Regulation 1924/2006. Norwegian also promised "sunn vektnedgang" (healthy weight loss) in two places.

This broke the project's own hard line in two of five languages, on the market with our second-highest impressions. Rewritten by a native editor to match the wording the other three languages already use — 46 replacements across 9 files, with all 65 FAQ pairs kept word-for-word identical between the visible text and the structured data.

Live check after deploy: zero occurrences remaining on either market.

5 · Backlinks — including two that were silently dead

Two of the existing backlinks never worked at allfixed

Both write.as assets from the first wave never rendered. The page shows raw **bold** and [anchor](url) as plain text — meaning zero actual links. So wave 1 was 5 backlinks, not 6, and the roundup page meant to get them all crawled was passing nothing.

The original check only looked for the web address as text on the page — it never confirmed a real link tag existed. I have rebuilt the roundup properly and it now points at all 5 surviving articles plus the new English one.

Three new backlinks, aimed at the page Google won't crawllive

/buy/ is still the one page Google has never fetched, so both English links point straight at it. New this wave: an English price breakdown on rentry.co, a German piece on what can and cannot actually be bought, and a Danish piece on the TV rumour. All anchors verified as real links.

Worth knowing: every telegra.ph link is rel="nofollow" — those pages help Google discover our pages but pass no authority. rentry.co gives a proper follow link, so future waves should favour it.

Correction: our indexer cannot index backlinksnote

The Indexing API only accepts URLs on domains we own, and we do not own telegra.ph or rentry.co. The only way to get a third-party backlink crawled is crawl-bait — a page that links to it — which is what Link-Spotter does and what I rebuilt today.

6 · Three things I did not touch — your call

Our review markup is self-servingneeds decision

Since the "RetaFit Official" pivot we are the brand's affiliate, and Google's rules exclude review snippets written by the seller or its affiliate. The test still passes — which is exactly why this hasn't surfaced — but the risk is a manual action that wipes the rich results we just earned across 9 pages. Fix is to drop the review block and keep the offer block. The visible "4.7 / our verdict" copy can stay.

The struck-through prices don't correspond to anythingneeds decision

The crossed-out £229 and £152 (and their euro/kroner equivalents) match no real price — they look back-solved from the store's percentage claims. Five bottles at the single-bottle price is £369.25, not £229. The copy then says the bundle "saves £100 versus single bottles" when the real difference is £240.25. Norwegian claims 1559 kr against a true 4125 kr. Danish is the only one that computes correctly.

Under UK and EU pricing rules a struck-through price must be a genuine former price. This is precisely the consumer-watchdog exposure that got Ozem+ listed. Safest fix: drop the fake anchors and keep the store's real percentages and true per-bottle prices, which are all correct.

Five filled stars against a 4.7 ratingneeds decision

Ten pages render ★★★★★ next to a stated 4.7, plus "loved across the UK & EU" — when no real customer reviews exist anywhere. Should show 4.7 stars and be labelled clearly as our editorial view.
